Under King John the nations most powerful barons decided some restrictions over an absolute monarchy were needed. They had been discussing this for some time and had met with the Archbishop of Canterbury, Stephen Langton, on at least two previous occasions in 1213 and 1214. The result was The Great Charter – Magna Carta – largely drafted by the Archbishop on their behalf. This was to become, along with the Domesday Book of 1086, one of the two most influential documents in English history with an impact on our law, constitution and human rights lasting until the present day. And two Lincolnshire notables were present when the king put his seal to it –Archbishop Langton and Bishop Hugh of Lincoln!

Nearby is Langton Manor romantically set within its moat. The present building dates from 1841 but stands on the site of earlier manor houses and may occupy the site of Stephen Langton’s birthplace. The surrounding fields (either side of the road by the information board) show traces of the mediaeval Langton village and ridge and furrow fields.
